1898

In 1898 three people died of the plague in Vienna. The last plague outbrake before that lay over 150 years in the past. Additionally the cases were not caused by natural contagion but were due to a laboratory accident. No wonder this incident sparked general fear and heated media coverage. Still there is more to this story worth discovering.

An Inconspicuous Entry

We start our search at the Church of the Holy Trinity of the Friars Minor in the Alservorstadt. This church is located next to the old General Hospital of Vienna and in the registers of the church we find an inconspicuous entry for a Mr Barisch who died of alleged blood decomposition. In fact he was the first of three plague fatalities caused by a laboratory accident in the nearby hospital.

Plague Cultures from India
Towards the end of the 19th century, the great plague epidemics in Europe had long been a thing of the past. However, a major epidemic was spreading again in Asia and Bombay was one of the Epicenters in the late 1890-ies.
Advancements in Bacteriology
Around the same time bacteriology was an emerging and rapidly growing field of science. The bacillus causing the plague had just been discovered in 1894. With many questions still to be answered and a realistic threat of the epidemic spreading to Europe as well, the Academy of Sciences in Vienna decided to send an expedition of 3 doctors, 1 assistant and 1 servant to Bombay. Dr. Hermann Müller, some 31 years young, took the lead.
Austrian Mission Arrives in Bombay
The Austrians were full of zest for action and the first foreign plague mission to reach India.
Arthur Road
British Administration Officers invited them to set up their basecamp at Arthur Road Hospital - still today a center for infectious diseases - and in a school building adapted as a laboratory. The course of the disease was observed in the infected patients and various tests were carried out. This also included animal experiments to investigate the mode of infection.
Close Contact with the Plague
Despite close contact with sick people and only rudimentary hygiene, all participants returned safely to Vienna in May 1897, bringing with them cultures of plague bacillus to continue experimental research in Vienna. And this is where things get messy...
The Laboratory
After a short vacation, the next task was to find a workplace that was as isolated from the outside world as possible and suitable for the investigations.
The Bacteriological Institute
The only feasible place was the pathological institute of Vienna General Hospital. There they occupied a two-window room paved with stone slabs, which was not connected to any other study.
Plague Room
In this so-called "plague room" the research team conducted their experiments and also kept the infected laboratory animals. The working conditions for the investigations were therefore less than ideal.
Animal Experiments
At the time it was unknown which animals could contract the disease. Therefore the research team infected guinea pigs, rabbits, rats, mice, dogs, hyenas, cats, pigs, monkeys, birds, snakes, lizards, and frogs and conducted overall more than 750 animal experiments to find out how the bacterium enters the organism and how immunization against the plague could be achieved.
The Sick Rat Keeper
At least they could count on the help of the experienced and reliable servant of the Institute of Pathology and Anatomy Franz Barisch, who was well aware of the dangers of his position as an animal keeper.
Ratkeeper Barisch Gets Sick
It was around the time of the last scheduled experiments with only one guinea alive that Barisch contracted pneumonic plague.
Dr. Müller Orders Quarantine
Although Dr. Müller officially diagnosed him with influenza he must have had a feeling about the true cause of the illness because he transferred the patient into isolation in the General Hospital and had his flat disinfected.
Nurses Care for Barisch
Two nurses held the position at the bedside of the quickly deteriorating Barisch and he died three days after showing the first symptoms in the evening of the 18th October 1898.
Postmortem Diagnosis
Only afterward came the shocking diagnosis: During the microscopic examination, they found the plague pathogen!
Preventing an Epicemic
With Barisch dead it slowly dawned on Dr. Müller and the leading personnel of the Hospital, that a veritable crisis was unfolding.
Fear of Contamination
The conditions of the isolation had been mediocre at best and the quarantine room had been directly neighboring two infirmaries.
The Franz Joseph Hospital
Only a couple of years earlier the Austrian Emperor had opened a new hospital specialized in infectious and epidemic diseases in the south of the city, much better suited for the treatment of illnesses requiring isolation.
Relocation
What should have started immediately was now hastily set in motion. The transfer of all activities to contain the outbreak to the Kaiser Franz Joseph hospital.
Albine Pecha Feels Sick
Two days later one of the nurses of the deceased Barisch, Albine Pecha, already had a high fever. Dr. Müller decided to join her and the other nurse, who was still well, in isolation in a small pavilion on the grounds of the Franz Joseph Spital. This hut was specifically designed to accept suspected cases of severe infectious diseases.
Dr. Müller Still Healthy
Upon arrival, Dr. Müller was feeling well and convened with the Doctors of the Franz Joseph hospital. He was amused by the strictness of the quarantine provisions and had no premonition of carrying the bacillus himself. He was heard saying. "I am not ill, the doctor is not infectious after all."
Moving into Strict Isolation
Nevertheless, each of the three moved into separate rooms in the isolation hut. The rooms were each connected to one other room housing a nurse for every patient. No one was allowed to leave except for Müller for his regular visits to the other patients.
The quarantine provisions were indeed of the strictest sort: Nobody and nothing was allowed to leave the isolation hut. Even the tableware stayed inside. The nurses placed pots on the lawn and servants poured the food in them and immediately left. Three guards watched the square outside the hut, which was delimited by ropes and no one except the leading doctor and designated servants were allowed to cross. Everything had to be disinfected immediately and the nurses had to wear masks, whenever interacting with the patients.
Media Coverage
Above all the newspapers had quickly gotten hold of the story with the first newspaper spreading the rumor in the morning edition of the next day and were now watching every step closely.
Drama
But shortly after their arrival the full tragedy and bleakness of the situation unfolded. Now also Dr Müller was ill and the laboratory confirmed plague bacilli in his sputum and that of Nurse Albine Pecha. A mere 70 hours after the first death, of the three people who had been working around Barisch, two seemed to be irretrievably lost, including the doctor, who was not even supposed to be "infectious" - a downright devastating realization!
1898
Now the situation is tense. The spread of the plague across Vienna was an imminent threat. The city goes into emergency mode. The General Hospital closes off the whole section where the infected had been lying and working. Next to the Franz Joseph Hospital workers are erecting plague barracks to house large numbers of infected people. The sewers are searched for dead rats. The Town Hall sets up an emergency committee and the general commotion is fuelled by ever more unconfirmed rumors in the newspapers.
1898
Tourists leave the city and neighboring countries inquire whether they should set in place quarantine measures for people entering from Vienna.
1898
In the meantime, an external doctor has to take over the care for the patients, as Dr. Müller is not capable anymore. The new doctor has to change clothes between every patient - all clothes are burned immediately - and he has to take baths in diluted disinfectants before and after his visits.
1898
Dr. Müller rejects every form of treatment fully aware of the hopelessness of his situation. All other patients, nurses, doctors, and most of the possible contacts of the infected, who had been brought into isolation too, are inoculated with several doses of plague serum procured from the Pasteur Institute in Paris.
1898
Müller’s state is rapidly worsening.
A doctor from the Clinic remembers their last conversation:
“Tell everyone to forgive me as I forgive them, and thank all your colleagues for their kindness, here as well as in the hospital and at the clinic. Give my regards to all gentlemen, but especially to your Primarius, Dr. Obermayer, and Hofrat Nothnagel.”
“Is there anything else you wish for?”
“No, I just want to rest and die peacefully.”
1898
A priest is sent to administer the last sacrament through the window.
Dr. Müller sits on the edge of the bed, his hands folded on his knees, unmoving, his gaze steady. At a loss for words, he nods his consent, strikes the cross, and taps his chest. Tears are in his eyes when the priest asks him if he forgives everyone as God forgives him.
1898
Aftermath
Dr. Müller died in the early morning of the 23rd of October 1898 at only 32 years of age not even two days after he was diagnosed.
1898
Albine Pecha’s fight lasted considerably longer, possibly due to the shots of the serum but certainly due to the care of the hospital. The doctors applied all treatments available at the time. She received oxygen from an apparatus that was set up outside the window of her cabin. While her overall state seemed to be better for a very short time, she died on the 31st of October.
1898
The bodies were put in wooden caskets, then in metal caskets, and then sealed off hermetically and buried within 24 hours.
1898
The isolation hut was completely disinfected and closed off for a prolonged period and everything from inside was sterilized and then burned regardless.
1898
The quarantine measures of the new Franz Joseph Hospital for infectious diseases proved very effective. All persons inoculated with the serum felt side effects but in the end, recovered and the spread was stopped entirely.
1898
Until today Franz Barisch, Hermann Müller, and Albine Pecha are remembered as the last plague victims of Vienna.
1898
No question, the whole episode was a tragedy and many aspects may sound familiar as we all remember the outbreak of COVID-19 all too well. But in the light of things to come, this whole story only seems like a small deviation from normal. Of course, only few people in Vienna at that time could even start to imagine that their city would enter a catastrophic era of the World Wars and diseases such as the Spanish Flu only twenty years later.
